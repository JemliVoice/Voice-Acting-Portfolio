Chapter Three

Chapter OneChapter TwoChapter FourChapter FiveChapter SixChapter Seven

My mom has kind of always been all I had. Grandparents lived across the country, Dad didn't have siblings, not even a half sibling. My mom did have a sister, but they weren't close when I was growing up. Mom even cut ties with her family when she left upstate New York. I don't know if I've got cousins. All this to say, even if I did have cousins, their last names wouldn't have been mine. Or rather, my father's.

There was only one Geoffe Lehman, and that was my father.

By far, perhaps the strangest thing I saw today was that particular name printed on a document, a record at my job--at this social media company's headquarters in the Silicone Valley. The last time I saw that very name in anything was back in New York, in our local newsfeed fourteen years ago. In the obituary.

I was really young, but the memory of losing him was significant. It was the first time I had an attack. It's not that I remember him dying; I wasn't even there. I was with my grandparents and aunt. Grandma was on the phone in the kitchen, looking tense. I watched her from the floor of the living room, and I remember my aunt being next to me. I don't recall what she was doing. It's vague, but I think we were playing a game. What I remember vividly, though, was grandma hanging up the phone and turning to grandpa, telling him something with the saddest expression I've ever seen on her.

What she said made my grandfather bolt out of his seat. "Good heavens!" The tone in his voice made me very scared.

"Gramp?" I called to him, concerned. My aunt picked me up and tried to calm me.

"Hey, Trinity, how about we go play something in the other room, what do you say?" She ran a hand over my hair, brushing it away from my face. My grandparents looked at me in the same way they did when they had to break the news of not being able to repair my favorite toy: my neon orange HoverBubble.

"Yes, dear, listen to Frankie." My grandma tried to smile, but it looked painful. My aunt was just about to carry me away, but my grandma called to her again. "Francine, come over for a word." My aunt set me down and pet my head again.

"Go on, I'll meet you in the other room. I'll just grab a snack from Gram for us, ok?" I didn't say anything, just looked up at her. She left me to meet my grandparents in the kitchen. I stayed where she left me in the hall, watching the wall in front of me turn into waves, sea foam spilling out onto the carpet. I was mesmerized. It scared me a little, but even at that young age I knew nothing was actually happening to the wall. Still, it really did look like waves from the ocean's shore. I knew if I reached out to touch it, my hand would get wet. I wanted to test that, but something had my feet anchored in place and all I could do was stare.

I never did get to test if the wall was made of ocean waves. Not long after she left to have a word with my grandma, Aunt Frankie approached me with a plate of sliced apples and a sad expression on her face. When I made myself turn away from the wall to get a better look at her, I could see that there was pity mixed with her sadness. I wondered what Gram told her. I was close enough to the kitchen that I should have picked up something from the conversation, but I was too mesmerized by the wall.

The wall--I turned back to see if there were still waves, but it was mostly wall. My mind, still remembering what I saw, tried to recreate it but the image wasn't very strong. Now it was more like somebody displaying an image with a projector onto the wall.

"Hey, let's go have some apples and watch some cartoons. Your mom is on her way to come get you," Frankie rested one hand on my shoulder and led me to her room. "There's uh...something important she needs to tell you," she let out a deep breath, "it's about your dad. I don't have all the details, but it's best if you hear it from your mom."

She gave me the apples and somberly turned the tv on. I cautiously took a seat on the beanbag chair, careful not to spill the apples from the plate as though all my presentiments resided in the apples and one wrong move would cause a maelstrom in my grandparents' home. Frankie sat on the edge of her bed, not looking at anything.

"Um...Aunt Frankie?" She hardly lifted her head in my direction. "Mom's coming?" I wasn't sure how to ask what I meant.

"Yes, baby, your mom is on her way. Please, enjoy the apples." It seemed she wasn't sure how to say what she meant, either. I nibbled on a slice and turned my attention to the tv.

The Backdoorigans was on. A group of five colorful bots ran out of a server rack and into a LCD screen. This was a transition scene, so I knew I missed the first half of the episode. The green one pulled out a digital flower patch and plugged it in. The flowers buzzed to life when they connected with the circuit-grass.

"Here's my part of making our cyberspace a beautiful place!" Everyone clapped at his proclamation. The flower patch twinkled.

"My turn now!" The pink one bellowed. She placed her HoverBubble over the flower, pulled out the gadget that pairs with it, which was a big red button that said in bubbly letters "Encrypt!".

"Ok, guys," she held the button out as she looked at each member of the group, "are we ready to encrypt our beautiful flower, so that no online-snoop can hack it?"

The four friends cheered, and they all pressed the button after a countdown. They performed a secret handshake, giggled, and then I heard another sound that did not come from the TV. I looked back over at my aunt, who also heard the melody coming from the walls. Mom was here.

I was the one who picked the sound for her. It was a simple, eight note tune that my mother would sing all the time. The start of some old song.

The contrast between hearing the cheerful tune of my mother's arrival and seeing her at the front door was whiplash.

Mom was so distraught. Her cheeks were blackened with mascara and wet from tears. Her eyes, a smudge of black and red. It horrified me to see her like this. I ran to her, about to cry myself.

"Mama? Why are you crying?" My throat knotted and tears were spilling from my eyes already. She turned away and let out a muffled sob.

"Go tell your aunt and grandparents goodbye." She managed to choke out.

I turned to them, sobbing. "What's going on?" My grandma reached out to me and held me in her arms. My aunt followed suit, and then so did Grandpa.

"We love you, and we are always right here, dear." My grandma's soft voice provided me little comfort. I still responded with an I love you too. One by one, they let me out of their arms and escorted me to my mom. She picked me up, carried me to the car and buckled me into my car seat.

My mother hadn't said anything to me since she instructed me to tell my family goodbye. I let the silence be, and watched our vehicle pass everything by. We were going so fast that trees outside blended into each other, and I was reminded of the wall turning into water in my grandparents' house. My mother's eyes were the ocean, and though I couldn't see her face, I could imagine it in my mind. Her tears created waves and the entirety of the world outside our car was water. Trees and passing cars were tides of the ocean. The sky itself, a cosmic tide spilling sea foam clouds to crash into the world of water below it.

The car turned, and I was greeted with a very familiar set of waves. I watched these waves turn into trees and mailboxes and flowers from neighbors gardens. Mom pulled into our driveway, parked, and sat still for a moment before turning to me.

"Trinity, you are so patient. Thank you for that." Mom's words were careful, deliberate. Her cheeks dried up but the black smudge remained. "I..." she started, but then looked away, back to stare at the windshield before her. "Did your grandparents or Frankie tell you?"

"Tell me what?" I heard her take a deep breath, and watched her shoulders hunch as she exhaled.

"I'm so sorry, Trinity." She took another breath and steadied herself. "It's your father...he...he's not coming home. I was at work when they informed me that he...he passed away." She choked on her last words. I was speechless, in a state of pure disbelief. Seeing my mom this way, I knew that she was telling the truth, but I couldn't comprehend it. Dad, not coming home, ever again? I saw him this morning at home; him and mom sipping coffee, my mom teasing him about his crooked tie...no, he had to be coming home. It was six o'clock, he had to be pulling into the driveway any minute now.

I desperately wanted to be back in Aunt Frankie's room, eating apples and watching my favorite cartoon, waiting for my mom and dad to pick me up. Anywhere but where my mind was at that moment.

The way I felt then reflected the way I feel now after reading that name on the document.
