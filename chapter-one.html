Chapter One

Chapter TwoChapter ThreeChapter FourChapter FiveChapter SixChapter Seven

Trash flits between my feet as I make my familiar walk to work. The wind's apathetic fingers pierce through my clothes and usher gray clouds overhead to cast a dull ambiance on the city below. The only colors in the world come from flashing billboards and graffiti that had been hastily scrawled on the mega who-knows-what-they-do business skyscrapers. Today, even the dazzling billboards are dulled by the smog. When I first moved here for my job, the unique displays of vandalism were my landmarks. All these gray towering buildings look the same, especially in this part of the city where I work. The busy, flashy billboards changed their ads every day, so I could never rely on them as I could graffiti. The CEO's either didn't know who to hire to clean them off, or they didn't know that they could easily run to the hardware store and pick up a graffiti cleaner spray. Or (I giggle to myself about this one) they really liked the vandalism on their buildings. Ultimately, the reason the graffiti never got scrubbed off was perhaps because the giant billboards, holograms, and ads took up their all of their sleazy attention.

I approach the end of the sidewalk, feeling good about leaving early for once. Without pausing my cadence, I toss a quick glance to my left and to my right before continuing onward, my mind occupied already with the day ahead. My FeedWatch buzzes against my wrist, and I excitedly check my notification.

Oh! Only three minutes until--

HOOONK--screeching tires, headlights-- the blaring noise alone startles my heart out of my body and I can't breathe--the world stops in this moment--

--until furious shouting jump-starts my heart. I regain the feeling of ground beneath my feet and out of sheer panic I pelt across the street to safety. I whip around to see the man in the electric car that nearly ran me over, still shouting and violently jabbing his hands in my direction. I huff at him and his stupid Feed display he chose for his car.

A glint in my glasses of something dark and twitching catches my eye and the moment I turn to get a better look, regret mixed with disgust fills my stomach. The smog itself seemed to cower away from the sight before me, and I could see clearly a black tentacle the size of a human bursting out of the sewer, sending the manhole cover flying out with enough force to kill someone. The tentacle thrashes, flinging slimy liquid all over the road. It isn't just thrashing--my stomach drops again as I realize it is also blindly grabbing at anything it can with venomous suction cups.

Gasping, I tear my eyes away from the scene and rummage through my bag for my phone. As soon as the smooth case of the phone brushes my fingers, I snatch it and immediately open the Cheese app. Insects of many legs scamper across the screen and up my arms, biting me--they crawl out of my ears and burrow into my scalp, my short hair not doing anything to stop them. They reek of disease, but I ignore them. Every nerve in my body screams at me to swat at them and rip my skin off. My fingers fly across the screen of my Feed as though my life depends on it.

In a way it did; it kept me from losing my mind completely. The Feed, as the doctor simplified it, acts as a way to calm the brain and anchor it to reality. The pictures from the Feed replace the negative ones that my brain makes up. It's incredible how far technology has come. The doctor did admit that though it was unconventional to "prescribe" social media for a case such as mine, we've found that it works--and I don't have to pay for expensive medication that's only been tested on monkeys so far. My specific situation is relatively under-researched.

Whenever I get overly stressed and upset I see things that aren't there, so I need the images of friends, cats, dogs, and cartoon characters to focus my brain on reality. Which is ironic considering the images are on a screen. I hadn't noticed when the insects disappeared, but they were gone. I gulp in deep breaths and check behind me again for black tentacles. To my satisfaction I found none.

Electric bikes whizz by me as I shakily start walking down the sidewalk again. People in suits on their way to work give me looks through scrunched eyebrows and frowns. I'm used to that now though. Well, if I'm being honest, I'm really not...it's just something that happens every time I have an attack. People stare. I can either accept that, or shout at them that 'I'm nuts! I'm downright mental! Got a problem with that?!' I've thought about doing the latter from time to time and it makes me laugh. I used to wish that they could see what I saw, but lately as my condition has gotten worse I'm actually glad that they don't and never will.

My heart is still racing, and since it was sidewalk the rest of the way, I allow myself to go through my Feed as I walked. It is only too soon that I reach the grand glass entry of Cheese Inc. Headquarters. I stuff my phone back into my bag as I push through the swivel doors.

"Trinity, only a minute late. Better than last time, but not good enough. Not nearly good
enough!" A man in a sharply pressed slate gray business suit approaches me as if he was standing around doing nothing but waiting for me. He didn't even look up from his phone to address me.

I cross my arms and narrow my eyes at him. "Gosh, Charles, I--"

"That's Mr. Pterygium to you. I did not hire you to speak to me or ask questions." Looking up from his phone, he snaps his fingers. "Get me a latte. My usual, obviously. There's lots and lots of things I need to do today." Without another word, he turns on his heel and saunters out of the lobby, waving and smiling at the employees going about the morning. He even pauses to flirt with a hurried and tired-looking colleague before heading to the elevators.

I roll my eyes behind his back. Yeah, you didn't hire a personal secretary to talk to you. Of course not. Well, better get to it.

After punching in on the lobby's front desk computer and wishing my colleagues a good morning, I head down the hall that leads employees to the elevators. At Cheese Inc., we are all about innovative technologies. My favorite part of the day is using the elevators, and while that might sound funny to most companies around us, we actually are the second business in our city to install the latest Individual Laminar Lift System, or for English-speakers, Personal Elevators. We found it was much easier to call them ILLIS, though. These are way better than the regular elevators, because instead of displaying general popular Feeds that cater to most people, ILLIS actually shows you your Feed and plays your music.

I scan my badge to call an ILLIS and wait for one to come down from the eighteenth floor. Of course, even though we have a fleet of them taking up the entire center of the building, I still have to wait for one that's coming from the eighteenth floor. It doesn't take long, though, before I can see a man on the phone behind the glass walls of the ILLIS, his Feed displaying charts and data. None of his Feed, however, covers his heated argument over the phone. The ILLIS lands, and as the doors open his voice floods out.

"Are you seriously telling me that Containment is down? Where is the director then?" He steps out of the ILLIS, but stands still as he receives words from the other person on the phone. His face is incomprehensible; twisted under a calm exterior, just barely keeping rage and panic at bay. "You know the Feed goes on with or without it, right? Do you understand the repercussions of what you're doing? Get containment back online now." The man is spitting, red in the face. "That's your department! I've got to contact the director. You're this close to being fired." Hanging up, he practically throws his phone in his pocket and storms off.

There is nobody else in the entire building for him. Eyes electric with focus, he strides past me, and would have knocked me down if I hadn't stepped out of his way. I shiver as the gush of air he funneled from his stride chilled me, and I turn to watch him go. I can't help but stare at him as the smoky dark cloud over his head left wisps of itself in the form of fog.

Containment? My heart sags inside me with anxiety. What the hell could a social media company be containing that would merit this type of response? My skin prickles with goosebumps, preparing to hear an alarm or something at any second. My thoughts swirl into another dark cloud, almost obscuring my vision entirely. I try to push through it, to act like things were normal and that was a completely normal phone call. The fog doesn't let up, though. Even though I know the ILLIS is in front of me, it seems like it is miles away in the darkness.

You know the Feed goes on with or without it. The phrase suddenly echoes through my thoughts. Containment.

Fire suddenly blinds me, no it's not fire--eyes. A pair of flaming eyes lock with mine and my stomach drops as out of the fog emerges a short, bulbous demonic being, screaming inside the elevator. I stumble back, tripping over my feet. My bag falls off my shoulder and I lunge for it, ripping out my phone from it. The howling coming out of the now flaming demon grew, the noise making my head pound from the pressure. I focus on my phone, ignoring all of it.

The light from my Feed illuminates the fog around me, and I blink a few times to adjust to the blooming light. I am a little surprised to see my mother smiling at me. I blink again and sure enough, there she was at the top of my Feed. I instantly feel relieved and allow my eyes to bore into the image. She looked beautiful. Her hair was styled in a way I have never seen on her, and she wore sparkling diamond earrings. I finally shift my eyes to her caption, which explained why she looked so nice.

"Twenty six years later and I still love you, maybe more now than when we met. Happy anniversary." My heart aches for reasons I can't explain. I didn't have a chance to dwell on it; a tiny cough from behind me felt like a splash of cold water to the face. I realize I was still sitting on the floor, at my place of work, and coworkers were giving me strange looks.

I grip my phone and straighten quickly, smoothing out my green slacks. The demon and the fog were gone, so I enter the ILLIS. The doors shut and my shaky finger presses the button for the common floor with the cafe. My phone connects with the elevator and images of my Feed and jewelry ads color the walls. The ILLIS fills with 4 Non Blondes' What's Up?  coming from the speakers.

Normally I would be jamming out, but not this time. I have too much in my head, what with my boss yelling at me, the conversation I overheard, my mom--and the monsters were just at bay, hidden behind pictures and music. I sigh, rubbing my temples. What is going on.

On the glass wall in front of me slides the picture of my mom with those sparkling earrings that mean so much to her, and with her hair up like that, I got to notice just how much she aged from the woman in my memory.

The passage of time seems more profound as I've grown. It feels silly to admit, but when I was young, I never thought that when I'd grow up, my mom would too. Not only that, but all those things that seemed normal, that I just accepted; in reality I was blind to how my mom felt about dad.

Looking back at the picture of her, I'm sure my dad would've probably looked at her in the same way I could imagine he looked at her on their wedding day. How her smile would have been genuine. But it's been fourteen years since he's been in their anniversary picture.

I'll call her during break, I decide.
